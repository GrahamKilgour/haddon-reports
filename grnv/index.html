COA Hearing Outcome — February 12, 2026

The Committee of Adjustment hearing for File B-25:094 was held on February 12, 2026. The application was tabled following Development Planning's recommendation to allow time for additional information and revisions. View Staff Report → Three City departments recommended Deny or Table:

  • Source Protection Planning (Deny): Cited a discrepancy between the Zoning By-law minimum lot size (0.80 ha, which all lots meet) and the Mid-Spencer Creek/Greensville Subwatershed Study minimum (0.82 ha for the Sandy Silt soil area). The 0.82 ha figure is derived from a MECP Procedure D-5-4 nitrate dilution model that assumes septic effluent reaches the water table — an assumption that does not apply where hydraulic isolation is confirmed. Phase 1 drilling has already identified over 19 metres of continuous low-permeability silt and clay (far exceeding the 10-metre MECP minimum for hydraulic isolation), and Phase 2 interference testing commencing late February 2026 is expected to provide definitive confirmation. If hydraulic isolation is confirmed, the D-5-4 model and its 0.82 ha lot sizing requirement become inapplicable, resolving this issue entirely. See Section 10.5 for full analysis of resolution paths.
  • Transportation Planning (Deny): The proposed shared driveway access to Crooks Hollow Road has three deficiencies: (1) The access point is too close to the Highway 8 intersection and does not meet the technical minimum for safety and operation; (2) The easement/mutual access runs within the 9.0-metre right-of-way dedication that the City requires along the Highway 8 frontage; (3) A 12.19 m × 12.19 m daylighting triangle is required at the northwest corner of Highway 8 and Crooks Hollow Road, through which no vehicle access will be permitted.
  • Natural Heritage (Table): An Environmental Impact Statement was not submitted with the consent application. Under Bill 23 (More Homes Built Faster Act, 2022), residential projects with fewer than 10 units are no longer subject to Site Plan control, meaning the EIS cannot be deferred to the Site Plan stage. Natural Heritage also raised concern that the current driveway layout would remove nearly all existing trees along the south property line, recommending the applicant explore alternative driveway configurations.

Five departments supported approval or had no concerns (Development Engineering, Building Engineering, Forestry, Zoning, Legislative Approvals). Development Engineering recommended Approve with Conditions, including submission of a hydrogeological report, a Consent Agreement, and driveway access technical documentation.

2.3 Stage 3: Site-Specific Assessment (AhHa-514)

Field Work: May 6 – October 28, 2024 (Detritus Consulting Ltd.)

Scope: 255 one-metre square test units excavated by hand across Site 1 (AhHa-514), covering an area approximately 145 m north–south by 206 m east–west (~4.7 ha archaeological study area). Note: AhHa-515 (Site 2) was assessed under a separate Stage 3 report and is not covered here. View Site 2 A&P Letter →

To facilitate assessment, AhHa-514 was divided into three areas:

  1. Locus 1 (pre-contact Indigenous lithic concentration) — 148 test units. Recovered 563 pre-contact Indigenous artifacts and 195 Euro-Canadian artifacts. The Indigenous assemblage comprised 561 pieces of chipping detritus, one biface (Ancaster chert), and one core (Onondaga chert). All lithic raw materials were Ancaster or Onondaga cherts. Sixteen test units yielded ten or more lithic artifacts, meeting the criteria for Stage 4 mitigation.
  2. Locus 2 (Euro-Canadian artifact concentration) — 50 test units. Recovered 474 Euro-Canadian artifacts and 13 pre-contact Indigenous artifacts (12 pieces of chipping detritus and one end scraper, Onondaga chert). Euro-Canadian ceramics date primarily to ca. 1820–1860, with a strong post-1830 emphasis. Over 80% of the assemblage predates 1870.
  3. Periphery (remainder of site outside Loci 1 and 2) — 57 test units. Recovered 113 Euro-Canadian artifacts and 19 pre-contact Indigenous artifacts. Low artifact densities confirmed site boundaries.

Total: 1,407 artifacts recovered and catalogued (802 Euro-Canadian, 605 pre-contact Indigenous).

Key findings:

  • The pre-contact Indigenous component is interpreted as a small to medium-sized activity area occupied briefly by Indigenous people (Detritus, §4.0). No temporally diagnostic artifacts were recovered. No Indigenous ceramics or fire-cracked rock were found during any stage of assessment.
  • The Euro-Canadian component is interpreted as a plough-disturbed domestic scatter associated with a residence located outside the Study Area, dating to the mid-19th century. The site was not the location of a home lot (Detritus, §3.4.1). The lack of faunal remains and the low density of structural materials support this interpretation.

Stage 3 Recommendations (four outcomes):

  1. Locus 1 — Indigenous component: Fulfils criteria for Stage 4 mitigation. Recommended for Avoidance and Protection (A&P) with a 25 m × 25 m protected area and minimum 10 m buffer, including fencing, construction drawing notation, and "no-go" instructions for all on-site personnel.
  2. Locus 2 — Euro-Canadian component: Fulfils criteria for Stage 4 mitigation. Recommended for Stage 4 Mechanical Topsoil Removal (MTR) to identify any subsurface cultural features.
  3. Locus 1 and Periphery — Euro-Canadian component: Does not fulfil criteria for Stage 4 assessment. No further archaeological investigation required.
  4. Locus 2 and Periphery — Indigenous component: Does not fulfil criteria for Stage 4 assessment. No further archaeological investigation required.

Stage 3 Report: Final report dated January 28, 2026 (Detritus Consulting Ltd.). Submitted to MCM; formal concurrence received January 2026.

2.4 Stage 4: Mitigation

Stage 4 Field Work (October 28 – November 22, 2024): 84 one-metre square test units excavated at AhHa-514 to further characterize the site and support the proposed mitigation strategy. Note: Stage 4 scope for AhHa-515 is addressed under the separate Site 2 assessment; details below pertain to Site 1 (AhHa-514) pending cross-reference against the final Stage 4 report.

Mitigation Approach — Avoidance and Protection (A&P):

Rather than conducting full archaeological excavation (which would be costly and time-consuming), the project employs an Avoidance and Protection strategy:

  • Indigenous component (AhHa-514): Long-term avoidance through protective buffer and restrictive covenant. The archaeological zone will be preserved in place beneath undisturbed land.
  • Euro-Canadian component (Locus 2): Mechanical Topsoil Removal (MTR) protocol for scattered Euro-Canadian materials that do not warrant full excavation.
  • Restrictive covenant: To be registered on title for Lot 1 specifically, restricting ground disturbance within the archaeological buffer zone. This is a permanent encumbrance that runs with the land.

Outstanding Deliverables:

  • Updated A&P plan mapping showing actual excavation areas (required per MCM's January 2026 concurrence letter before final A&P plan acceptance)
  • Finalized Stage 4 mitigation report for MCM submission
  • Stage 4 concurrence from MCM (anticipated Q1–Q2 2026)
  • Construction monitoring protocol: Licensed archaeologist required on-site during any ground disturbance within archaeological zones

Indigenous Consultation: Comprehensive Indigenous consultation was conducted throughout all archaeological stages, consistent with regulatory requirements and best practices.

4.2 Level 1 Assessment (December 2022)

The Level 1 Hydrogeological Assessment by Terra-Dynamics (authored by Jayme D. Campbell, P.Eng.) established the baseline understanding of the site's hydrogeological setting. View Level 1 Report →

Key Findings:

  • Overburden thickness from MECP well records: 26–49 m, averaging 33 m in the Greensville area
  • City of Hamilton monitoring well MW-06 (WWR 7040534) located near the site: 15.2 m (50 ft) of low-permeability material above an intermediate sand aquifer
  • The site "may qualify" for hydraulic isolation classification per MECP criteria (10 m of geologic material with K ≤ 10⁻⁷ m/s), pending additional field data
  • D-5-4 analysis using 150 mm/yr infiltration rate (per City of Hamilton Hydrogeological Study Guidelines for sandy silt soils) supports a maximum of 5 lots without hydraulic isolation, and potentially more with isolation confirmed

D-5-4 Lot Count Analysis:

  • Dilution area: 4.7 ha (total 5.0 ha minus 0.3 ha roads)
  • Infiltration rate: 150 mm/yr (Hamilton Guidelines, Table 4 — sandy silt)
  • Sewage loading: 1,000 L/day per lot (3-bedroom homes)
  • Nitrate-nitrogen effluent: 40 mg/L
  • Result: 5 lots = 8.9 mg/L nitrate (below 10 mg/L standard); 6 lots = 10.3 mg/L (exceeds standard)
  • Without hydraulic isolation, 5 lots is the absolute maximum. With isolation confirmed, the zoning constraint (0.8 ha minimum) rather than hydrogeology becomes the limiting factor.

Nitrate Assessment Waiver: Through technical consultation with MECP and City of Hamilton Source Protection Planning in February–March 2023, the Nitrate Assessment requirement was waived, subject to confirmation that the site meets MECP criteria for hydraulic isolation. This agreement secured the project's 5-lot density.

Below the 0.3 m topsoil layer, the borehole column consists of over 19 metres of continuous fine-grained confining material. Grain-size analysis using HydrogeoSieveXL determined the hydraulic conductivity of this material to be ≤ 10⁻⁷ m/s, meeting MECP 2008 standards for hydraulic isolation classification.

This far exceeds the MECP 10-metre minimum requirement. Regional well records indicate bedrock at 35–40 m depth, suggesting additional confining material extends well below the Phase 1 drilling depth of 19.66 m.

Phase 2 interference testing will provide definitive confirmation of aquitard integrity by simultaneously monitoring shallow well water levels during bedrock pumping. This testing is scheduled to commence the last week of February 2026 as part of a staged drilling program.

Secondary Finding — Intermediate Sand Aquifer Search: Despite the discovery of protective clay layers, the Phase 1 program did not identify a consistent Intermediate Sand Aquifer at the anticipated 15–17 metre depth with adequate yield for domestic water supply. This means the original strategy of sourcing clean shallow water (to avoid hydrogen sulphide treatment associated with bedrock water) cannot be implemented.

Strategic Pivot: The water supply strategy now targets the Lockport Formation bedrock dolostone aquifer at depth, accepting that water treatment for hydrogen sulphide will be required (see Section 4.5).

4.4 Bedrock Water Quality Context

Regional Bedrock Groundwater: The Level 1 Hydrogeological Assessment (December 2022) identified that the Lockport Formation bedrock aquifer underlying the Greensville Rural Settlement Area is known for elevated dissolved hydrogen sulphide ("rotten egg" odour) due to anoxic conditions and sulphate-reducing bacteria activity at depth.

Additionally, monitoring data from City of Hamilton well MW-06-D (the deep bedrock completion near the site) shows:

  • Antimony: 0.0072–0.0078 mg/L in 2015 and 2017, exceeding the MAC of 0.006 mg/L (not exceeded in 2020 sampling)
  • Fluoride: 1.57 mg/L in 2017, exceeding the criterion of 1.5 mg/L (not exceeded in 2015 at 1.43 mg/L)
  • Sulphate: Very high at 1,090–1,190 mg/L (aesthetic concern; treatment options limited)
  • Hardness: Very high at 1,120–1,350 mg/L (aesthetic concern)
  • Hydrogen sulphide: Not tested at MW-06 but identified as "a significant known concern for bedrock wells in the Greensville area" (Terra-Dynamics, 2007 regional study)

Overburden Water Quality (MW-06-S — shallow completion): The shallower overburden aquifer shows significantly better water quality — calcium-bicarbonate type, fresh, with only aesthetic concerns (iron, manganese, hardness). This is consistent with the general pattern: overburden water in the Greensville area is potable with minimal treatment, while bedrock water requires treatment.

Relevance to Development: Because the intermediate sand aquifer strategy is no longer viable and the water supply will target the bedrock Lockport Formation, all lots will require water treatment systems. This is standard practice for rural estate properties throughout the Greensville area, where hundreds of existing private wells utilize the same approach (see Section 4.5 for treatment details).

Water quality will be managed via standard residential treatment systems (activated carbon filtration and/or reverse osmosis) to remove aesthetic sulphur odor and taste parameters. Treatment system specifications will be provided to all lot purchasers as part of property disclosure documentation. Interference Testing Protocol: During Phase 2 pumping tests, Terra-Dynamics will conduct interference testing — the critical success factor for confirming hydraulic isolation:

  • Each bedrock well pumped at constant rate (~5–10 GPM estimated) for 6 hours
  • Manual water level measurements in MW-1 and MW-2 (shallow clay monitoring wells) recorded at pre-pumping baseline (1 hour static equilibrium), 15-minute intervals during first 2 hours, 30-minute intervals during hours 2–6, and 15-minute intervals during 1-hour recovery phase
  • Atmospheric pressure compensation and barometric correction applied to all measurements

Success Criterion: Shallow water levels in MW-1 and MW-2 remain unchanged (±0.05 metres = measurement uncertainty) during bedrock well pumping = definitive confirmation of aquitard integrity and hydraulic isolation.

  • Requirement for Complex Nitrate Assessment (loss of regulatory waiver)

  • Extended approvals timeline (additional 6–12 months)
  • Potentially increased development costs for enhanced water quality monitoring

Contingency Well Yield Scenarios: Based on regional bedrock aquifer productivity, well rehabilitation results from December 2025, and the staged drilling approach (2–3 new wells depending on BW-1 results):

  • Optimistic: 8–12 GPM per well (sufficient for 2–3 homes with standard demand)
  • Expected: 5–8 GPM per well (sufficient with cistern buffer system)
  • Conservative: 2–5 GPM per well (requires cistern buffer plus water conservation measures)

All scenarios are acceptable for rural estate residential use per Ontario Building Code standards.

Cistern Buffer Strategy (if well yields are \<5 GPM):

  • System design: Above-ground or buried cistern storage (500–1,000 gallons capacity)
  • Operation: Low-yield well continuously refills cistern over 24-hour period; cistern supplies household demand during peak use
  • Standards compliance: Accepted standard for rural estate lots per Ontario Building Code; widely used in Grand River watershed area
  • Maintenance: Annual tank inspection; sediment flushing; water quality testing annually

10.4 Key Technical Achievements

Hydraulic Isolation — Strong Phase 1 Evidence: Phase 1 drilling at MW-2 penetrated 19.66 m entirely through continuous low-permeability silt and clay, far exceeding the MECP 10-metre minimum. Formal documentation for hydraulic isolation classification is being prepared by Terra-Dynamics for City submission. Phase 2 interference testing during BW-1 pumping will provide definitive confirmation, with results to be presented to the City at the next Committee of Adjustment meeting. If confirmed, the Nitrate Assessment waiver secured in early 2023 will be formalized, securing the project's 5-lot density.

Blended Archaeological Mitigation Strategy — Efficient and Compliant: Stage 4 mitigation combines avoidance and long-term protection for the Indigenous component with targeted mitigation excavation for Euro-Canadian components, reducing disruption while maintaining full regulatory compliance.

Existing Well Rehabilitation — Infrastructure Optimization: Successful rehabilitation of existing 160-foot bedrock well enables integration into Phase 2 testing and monitoring program, providing baseline reference data for the 2–3 new production wells.

10.5 Risk Mitigation Strategies

Regulatory Pathway: The application tabling, while adding timeline, is a constructive outcome. The Committee did not refuse the application — it requested additional information. The 12 proposed conditions and specific departmental feedback provide a clear roadmap to approval. Five of eight departments supported the application or had no concerns.

Transportation Access Redesign: The access design revision, while adding cost and timeline, may produce a superior site plan by addressing tree removal concerns simultaneously. The driveway relocation creates an opportunity to coordinate with Natural Heritage on a design that preserves more of the southern boundary tree cover.

Lot Size Resolution: All lots comply with the legally binding R2-14 Zoning By-law minimum of 0.80 ha. The Subwatershed Study's 0.82 ha recommendation is derived from a MECP Procedure D-5-4 nitrate dilution model (Figure 10.4.5) that assumes septic effluent migrates to a vulnerable shallow aquifer. This assumption does not hold where hydraulic isolation is confirmed. Phase 1 drilling at MW-2 identified 19.66 m of continuous low-permeability silt and clay — nearly double the 10-metre MECP minimum for hydraulic isolation classification. Phase 2 interference testing is expected to provide definitive confirmation, resolving this issue through the strongest available technical path. Additionally, the Subwatershed Study itself provides that smaller lot sizes may be proposed subject to "approval of applications under the Planning Act" (Section 10.4.5.2, p. 365), establishing that the 0.82 ha figure is a guideline informed by site-specific conditions, not an absolute regulatory limit. Fallback options, should they be needed, include: (A) minor lot boundary adjustments from the oversized retained parcel (1.8164 ha — 0.99+ ha above the 0.82 ha threshold), which could provide the additional 134–189 sq m needed per severed parcel; (B) a technical response demonstrating that the D-5-4 model inputs (3-bedroom dwelling, 10% impervious surface) overestimate actual loading for the proposed development; or (C) negotiation with Source Protection Planning informed by the Phase 2 hydrogeological results.

Hydrogen Sulphide Water Treatment: Presumptive treatment approach disclosed proactively in final report, positioning responsibility with future homeowners rather than creating regulatory approval barrier. Standard practice for rural estate properties in the Greensville area.

Low Well Yield Contingency: Cistern buffer system approach identified as contingency if bedrock wells yield \<5 GPM; provides accepted standard for rural estate lots.

Hydraulic Isolation Confirmation: Phase 2 interference testing during the staged drilling program will provide definitive data on aquitard integrity. Phase 1 findings are supportive, with over 19 metres of continuous low-permeability silt/clay identified at MW-2, far exceeding the 10-metre MECP minimum. Regional hydrogeology literature and nearby municipal well records document consistent aquitard presence throughout the Greensville area. Results will be compiled and presented to the City of Hamilton at the next Committee of Adjustment meeting.
